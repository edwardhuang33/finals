Determinants of optimal monetary policy

  • Note that the combining the three equations gives an equation for real interest rates:

which can be shifted forwards one period to give next period's rate

  • Hence magnitude of rate change depends:
      • More powerful monetary transmission means rates raised less

    • Steepens PC but also flattens MR
  • Uncertainty
    • 'False moves' by Central Banks generate inefficient volatility, hence uncertainty limits policy interventions
      • Strength of dampening effect dependent on level of uncertainty and CB aversion to making 'false moves'
    • Uncertainty over parameters also limits policy invention (Brainard principle)

 

Note on the Taylor rule

  • In standard model there is no output gap term as the inflation dynamics already incorporates all the information about output
  • But if there was a lag in the Phillips Curve (i.e. takes 1 period for output gap to impact inflation) then incorporating this into the model would generate a Taylor rule-like equation

Extension 2: Rational expectations

 

  • Consider positive demand shock
    • Hence this expectation is irrational

 

Implications:

  • Improved welfare relative to adaptive expectations
  • CB does not vary monetary policy in response to a demand shock
    • Could conceivably undertake stabilising monetary policy if it had private info on a future shock, but the alternative strategy of releasing the info to the private sector would be equally effective
  • Inflation reduction achieved without loss of output ("painless")
  • Rewritten PC implies output gap driven only by errors in private sector expectations

  • If there is perfect information access then fluctuations should be random

 

Limits:

  • Individual agents may lack info or cognitive ability to form RE
    • If there are irrational agents present then rational agents should factor this in, which adjusts their expectations upwards and hence weakens the RE effect further
  • Requires credibility and strong beliefs about others' behaviour
  • Stick prices and wages can prevent immediate return to equilibrium

 

Rational expectations in use:

  • Generally not used as a full model driving policy decisions, but some elements can be observed
    • After one-off changes in price level, CBs often keep policy constant knowing agents will identify the shock
    • Reduced macro volatility 1984-2007 often attributed to RE and credible targets
    • CBs refer to surveys of expected inflation in justifying policy decisions, and attempt to signal future policy and commitment to target inflation
  • Accepted in theory research by many Keynesians
