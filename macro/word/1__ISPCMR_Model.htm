Note on the Taylor rule

         In standard model there is no output gap term as the inflation dynamics already incorporates all the information about output

Implications:

         Improved welfare relative to adaptive expectations

         CB does not vary monetary policy in response to a demand shock

o    Could conceivably undertake stabilising monetary policy if it had private info on a future shock, but the alternative strategy of releasing the info to the private sector would be equally effective

         Inflation reduction achieved without loss of output ("painless")

         Rewritten PC implies output gap driven only by errors in private sector expectations

o    If there is perfect information access then fluctuations should be random

 

Limits:

         Individual agents may lack info or cognitive ability to form RE

o    If there are irrational agents present then rational agents should factor this in, which adjusts their expectations upwards and hence weakens the RE effect further

         Requires credibility and strong beliefs about others' behaviour

         Stick prices and wages can prevent immediate return to equilibrium

 

Rational expectations in use:

         Generally not used as a full model driving policy decisions, but some elements can be observed

o    After one-off changes in price level, CBs often keep policy constant knowing agents will identify the shock

o    Reduced macro volatility 1984-2007 often attributed to RE and credible targets

o    CBs refer to surveys of expected inflation in justifying policy decisions, and attempt to signal future policy and commitment to target inflation

         Accepted in theory research by many Keynesians
