Application: Sovereign Wealth Funds in Oil-Rich Nations

Allocate any temporary boost in oil revenue (stemming from volatile prices) into an SWF, so the dividend from oil is shared equally between current and future generations

Accumulate foreign financial assets when oil revenue is high, live off interest once oil runs out

 

If oil price remains low, fund should not be accessed: govt. must raise taxes or cut spending

 

Note that, even if fund does not exist, a CB that is controlling the FX market by accumulating foreign reserves is a de facto fund

 

Problems (mostly in implementation):

  SWF may be raided for political reasons

  Incentive for politicians to redirect oil revenue into illiquid, partisan investment projects

 

4.    Signalling Political Ability (Rogoff, 1990)

Voters know more about their individual taxes and public services they receive, than the overall government's fiscal position

 

If there is a politician who is more capable at providing public services cheaply, then there is incentive to choose higher spending and lower taxes now to signal their ability

Similar to more productive works spending on higher education to signal their ability

 

Similarly, if there is a politician who is more capable at cutting future wasteful spending, incentive to lower taxes today to indicate such

 

Both ⇒ current debt bias, can either lead to cycles in fiscal policies (if voters eventually realise the politician's true fiscal preferences) or persistent debt bias (if they don't)

 

5.    Finance Minister Behaviour

Conservative Finance Minister will use downward-adjusted estimate of next year's GDP to make budget

⇒ accumulation of precautionary buffers over time (although threat of being raided by spending ministers)

 

May also be due to inadequate information ⇒ overly optimistic forecasts / misinterpreted govt. accounts

 

6.    Delayed Stabilisation

If there is disagreement over how to divide burden of deficit reduction (e.g. different departments conflicting on how austerity should be implemented), there is incentive for each party to delay to signal how costly it is for them to accept reform

 

The less costly it is for a group to accept more of the austerity burden, the sooner they fold as the costs of conceding < costs of continued delay

 

Hence ⇒ persistent deficit

 

APPLICATIONS

1.    Developing Economies

External aid should not provide temporary relief from unsustainable debt situation: this delays the necessary stabilisation

 

Effective foreign aid should be conditional on implementation of stabilisation policies, and shrink if these policies are postponed

 

2.    Developed Economies

Evidence to show countries that run large deficits have weak governments with many conflicting parties (causality is debated)

 

Generally, more evidence for common-pool problems and deficit persistence than strategic deficits

 

         Tackling Debt Bias

Implement strong Minister of Finance, reduce number of conflicting parties

 

Fiscal rules:

Fiscal councils (e.g. OBR in UK)

i.e. semi-independent bodies tasked with ensuring fiscal policy is sustainable over long-run

Mandates may be to produce forecasts on growth, output gap, public finances, positive policy analysis, normative recommendations

Cost of Debt and Default

 

Costs of Sustainable Deficits

         In the (likely) event that Ricardian equivalence does not hold, there are adverse welfare effects:

o    Future distortionary taxes reduce labour supply, output, and consumption

o    Fewer public goods and/or higher taxes for future generations

o    Depressed wages and higher real interest rates redistributes from workers to capitalists

         Crowding out

o    Under Ricardian equivalence, ↑ government debt ⇒ one-to-one ↑ private savings (to pay off higher taxes in future), hence no crowding out

o    If private savings do not rise by enough (e.g. differing time horizons):

⇒ ↓ capital stocks with ↑ real interest rates

⇒ long-run 'crowding out'

i.e. public borrowing dampens private investment

o    Magnified if:

   Private saving is mostly to fund consumption in retirement (not passed on to future generations)

   Savings not sensitive to changes in interest rates

 

Costs of Unsustainable Deficits

i.e. fiscal actions do not satisfy government's present value budget constraint

         Some options:

a.       Painful adjustment into sustainability

Raise taxes / shrink public sector

b.      Default

c.       Money financing

Have until now assumed government cannot increase money supply, but doing so in medium/long run can increase inflation (e.g. German hyperinflation)

         Sudden changes to deficits ⇒ large costs that can scar economy, disruption of capital markets

         Unsuccessful attempts to cut deficits / reduce inflation may lead to higher inflation due to larger debt services run up during process

Low Interest Rates & Debt

         Risk-free interest rates have been < GDP growth rate for several years

⇒ no fiscal cost of rolling debt over

         Crowding out effect may still be present but likely to be small as risk-adjusted rate is likely low if risk-free rate is low (so effect on investment will be small)

o    MPK has been observed to be lower than risk-adjusted rate so less crowding out (since investment is dependent on MPK rather than risky rate)

 

Types of Government Debt

         Maturity structure

o    According to efficiency theory, the return on long-run debt = long-run interest + expected capital gains = short-run interest over same period

⇒ if long-run rate > short-run rate, price of bond expected to fall over time

         Indexation

o    Some bonds are indexed to inflation, so cost of inflation borne by government (incentive to avoid inflation)

 

Countries are less at risk from having unsustainable public debt if:

         They have marketable assets (e.g. oil, equity)

         They have a longer maturity structure on their debt

         Most of their debt is in their own currency

 

When Do Defaults Occur?

Two scenarios:

1.      Strategic

Political cost of high taxes / low spending

> political cost from defaulting on debt holders

+ costs from being unable to borrow for some time

 

More likely to be true if large proportion of debt is owned overseas

2.      Forced

Even if public debt is sustainable, if government cannot roll over debt:

         Default if debt is in foreign currency

         Monetary financing if debt is in domestic currency

Note hence that domestic currency debt means default risk is low - perhaps why most debt crises post-2008 have been in Eurozone

 

Recall that even a small probability of default ⇒ risk premium ⇒ ↑ cost of debt ⇒ ↑ probability of default (vicious circle)

The Balance Sheet Approach to Crises

(new in 2019 lectures)

 

Traditional crises models focus on flows (e.g. ) - the primary focus of this section

 

A new understanding focuses on changes in demand for domestic-currency-denominated financial assets.

Motivation:

o    Scale of capital outflows may be much larger than 'flow' fundamentals pre-crisis

o    Crisis might originate in private sector liabilities (concealed in government-focused 'flow' approach)

o    Problems in one sector may spill over, triggering balance-of-payments crisis

Leading indicator:

Financial fragility between sectors (measured via balance sheets)

 

Four balance sheet risks

1.      Maturity mismatches

Long-term assets and short-term liabilities might ⇒ crisis if market refuses to roll over short-term debt

Can occur in domestic or foreign currency

2.      Currency mismatches

Volatility in FX ⇒ capital loss

Hedging, if not universal, simply transfers risk to other sectors

3.      Capital structure mismatches

Excessive reliance on debt over equity ⇒ ↑ exposure to revenue shocks since debt is non-contingent

Undercapitalised banks and other institutions can trigger major crises

4.      Solvency issues

i.e. assets < liabilities

May occur due to excessive leverage or investment in low-yield assets

 

Balance sheet crises

Mechanisms driving economic downturn from balance sheet problems:

         ↑ demand for foreign assets ⇒ ↑ resources allocated to purchasing abroad ⇒ worsening current account

         Capital losses ⇒ negative wealth effect + credit crunch ⇒ ↓ AD

Often this leads to initial output overshooting as GDP drops hugely initially from worsening AD & CA, but then recovers from improvement in competitiveness through devaluation

 

Governments may attempt to alleviate balance-sheet crises by directly taking on private liabilities / guaranteeing against losses (e.g. fiscal bailouts of banks), but this simply transfers risks to public sector
