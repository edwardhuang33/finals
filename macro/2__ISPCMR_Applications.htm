3.2 Extensions

         Lessening the ZLB effect

o    Pigou argues that in a liquidity trap falling prices will ultimately stabilise as real value of private assets increases, creating a positive wealth effect that shifts IS to right

         Worsening the ZLB effect

o    In economies with an insufficiently large stock of private assets (e.g. debtor economies), IS curve may shift left

o    Hysteresis during deep recessions may mean permanent damage is inflicted by a failure to stabilise a temporary shock

e.g. capital scrapping (PS shift), long-term unemployed lose skills / stop seeking work (WS shift)

 

3.3 Policy at ZLB

 

A.    Forward guidance

CB keeps interest rates lower for longer, lowering long rates which may not be at ZLB even if short rates are

 

Note n-period interest rate is given by

 

Lower long rates affect private consumption and investment as funding for mortgages and corporate loans are priced relative to the government bond yield

Hence lower long rates mean cheaper financing for banks, firms and households

This is represented by a shift of IS to the right

This is dependent on rational expectations in financial markets, which is more plausible than for the wider economy

 

Time inconsistency

Forward guidance requires CBs to commit to low rates if the immediate effect is insufficient to eradicate demand deficit

e.g. Fed vowed to keep rates at ZLB for period after full employment was reached, BoE linked forward guidance to 2-year inflation forecasts < 2.5%

 

But committing is time inconsistent as this will require CB to raise inflation even when bliss point is achieved

This may be an issue due to outside pressures:

                                       i.            Accusations of currency manipulation

                                    ii.            Pensioners complaining savings are being taxed away

                                  iii.            Problems of asset price bubbles or zombie firms

 

B.    Price path targeting

Maintaining price path commits CB to 'irresponsible policy', locking in lower long-term real rates

 

PP target induces for any fall in , which immediately cuts real interest rates

Compare to traditional policy where the delay in monetary stimulus can realise adverse demand multipliers as businesses shut down and workers lose jobs, requiring larger future rate cuts

 

PP target also forces CBs to act symmetrically

Slow adjustment when transfers real income from borrowers to savers when contracts are based on assumption - the latter have lower consumption propensity so failures in credit market can mean overall demand drops

 

Effectiveness depends on:

a.       Expectations of low future nominal rates → ↓ current long rates

Dependent on RE in financial sector (passes effect onto mortgage/corporate markets

Relatively good evidence

b.      Expectations of higher future inflation → ↓ current long rates

Dependent on RE in consumer/corporate sector (borrowers must infer current loan offers more attractive)

Mixed evidence - consumption is somewhat more influenced by nominal rates than real rates in short term

 

Non-Linear Phillips Curve

Convex PC may occur due to obstacles to deflation e.g. nominal wage cuts resisted by workers

 

Falls in output → smaller ↓ , limiting rise in and hence smaller ↓ long rates

Doesn't nullify PP effect (unless completely elastic PC to left of VPC), but does weaken mechanism and increase reliance on RE in consumer sector

 

Stagflation

During Great Recession UK inflation remained above target due to rising import, energy, and food prices, so a CPI PP would have induced rise in long rates

 

Hence need to specify PP in terms of refined measure excluding stagflation driver - this is difficult and a little arbitrary, though

 

C.    Nominal GDP targeting

Supposedly retains advantages of PP target, but avoids problems with stagflation shocks

 

Issues:

o    Nominal GDP only observed quarterly, unlike monthly for CPI

o    Nominal GDP often revised several years after event (risk of false signals)

o    Nominal GDP excludes imports and includes exports, so if it is CPI that ultimately matters (living cost for households) then it may be an inaccurate measure

 

Issues with path targeting:

Control errors in inflation process

Inflation is not perfectly controlled by CB, so CB prefers simplest possible adjustment path

 

 

At C risk of positive cost-push shock causing overshoot that undermines credibility

At D risk a shock causes deflation that can mean economy hits ZLB

 

Public acceptance

Trade unions may campaign against policy to reach D once E is reached - path targeting may worsen relations with wage-setters

 

Supply shocks

Committing to future inflation will not restore output if recession reflects adverse supply shock

e.g. Following Great Recession

         Less lending → ↓ investment → ↓ capital stock

         Output in financial services has declines and re-regulation may prevent rebound

         Sectors dependent on exporting to Eurozone may have faced demand slump, where it takes time to find other export destinations

 

D.    Quantitative Easing

Creation of new electronic money to purchase government and corporate bonds

Boosts money supply and private liquidity whilst (theoretically) leaving net worth of financial sector unchanged

If QE is only reversible slowly, then this locks CB into higher future inflation since additional money supply cannot be withdrawn quickly once reached

 

Credibility?

o    Limited evidence for effectiveness of QE

   Has boosted narrow money supply but little evidence to suggest bank lending has increased

o    Monetarists argue QE is not aggressive enough as CBs can retrieve money (Friedmanite helicopter drop or buying and cancelling government bonds)

   Fears of hyperinflation as in early 20th century
