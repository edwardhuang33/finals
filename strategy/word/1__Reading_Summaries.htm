STRATEGY: READING SUMMARIES

Edward Huang

 

Barney (1991)

RBV

TL;DR: VRIN resources generate SCA

Kraaijenbrink, et al. (2010)

RBV Critiques

 

TL;DR: VRIN/O stands up to some critiques, but there are three notable exceptions: overly-narrow explanation of SCA (!N && !S), definitions of 'value' and 'resource' are too indeterminate

 

1.    No managerial implications: no obligation to, only explains SCA

2.    Infinite regress: in practice, rarely above second-order capability, only relevant in positivist approach

3.    Limited applicability

4.    SCA impossible

5.    Not theory of firm

6.    VRIN/O not necessary nor sufficient: empirical evidence has limited support for RBV; managerial capabilities also essential

7.    Resource value too vague: tautological, requires external determination of value

8.    Definition of resource unworkable: overly inclusive, undifferentiated resource types

Schmidt & Keil (2013)

Idiosyncratic Resource Value

TL;DR: Resources' value can be derived ex ante by examining: firm's market position, resource base (complementarities), interorganisational network position, prior knowledge & experience → managerial & demand-side role in RBV. Uncertainty and information asymmetry mean managerial judgement and idiosyncratic expectations mean there is a role for managers in SCA.

Powell (1995)
TQM

TL;DR: Explicit TQM schemes (eg. quality training, benchmarking) rarely produce SCA, but some tacit, imperfectly imitable features can (e.g. open culture, employee empowerment, executive commitment)

Rumelt (1991)

How much does industry matter?

TL;DR: corporate 1%, industry 8%, business-unit 46%. Manufacturing only (lower-than-average industry effects), 4 years from 1974

McGahan & Porter (1997)

How much does industry matter, really?

TL;DR: corporate 4%, industry 19%, business-unit 32%. All economic sectors, longer time period (14 years from 1981), differencing → stable effects only

Priem & Butler (2001)

Is the resource-based "view a useful perspective for strategic management research?

TL;DR: the resource-based view: is tautological; is too broad since many configurations can lead to success; neglects the role of product markets; has limited prescriptive abilities (black-boxes implementation)

Barney (2001)

Is the resource-based "view a useful perspective for strategic management research? Yes

TL;DR: all definitions are somewhat tautological, what matters is if they can certifiably parameterise; competitive advantage depends on definition; 1986 work develops factor/product model; RBV can help firms identify resources, find their strengths + imitate others

Wiggins & Ruefli (1995)

SCA

TL;DR: data on 20 years of firms in i) office & computing, ii) auto parts, iii) Fortune 500 industries reveal that: SCA can exist but are v. rare in long term, are not stable but are correlated with efficiency. Hypercompetition effects are observed. → ↑ RBV

Thomas & D'Aveni (2009)

Hypercompetition and volatility

TL;DR: in US manufacturing, there has been a sharp increase in within-industry heterogeneity of returns 1950-2002: ↑ volatility → shift towards hypercompetition. Changing border between dynamic capability and disruptive innovation

Coff (1999)

RBV Rents

TL;DR: SCA doesn't always → ↑ performance if rents are appropriated by stakeholders. With firm as nexus of contracts, ↑ internal bargaining power vs investors can lead to appropriation of rent e.g. tacit knowledge → ↑ bargaining power for managers and workers

Prahalad & Hamel (1990)

The core competence of the corporation

TL;DR: top managers must identify the core strengths (resources) and ensure structure is organised around harnessing these competencies most efficiently

Teece (1997)

Dynamic capabilities

TL;DR: dynamic capabilities == processes, asset positions and path dependencies for creating value in changing environments. Depends on demand stability, I & N from RBV

Teece (2014)
Dynamic capabilities revisited

TL;DR: DC esp. relevant for hypercompetitive environments. Differentiate between ordinary capabilities (admin) and dynamic (higher level that govern ordinary direction). SCA requires DC, RBV and strategy (latter two aligned by former)

Leiblein (2011)

RBV and DC

TL;DR: RBV vs DC vs strategic factor. Critique of RBV & DC mostly due to imprecise definition of resources in empirical studies (also makes meta-analyses of limited use)

Felin & Powell (2016)
Designing for DC

TL;DR: case study of Valve. Schemes should focus on highly integrative mechanisms with highly differentiated responses to environment. e.g. polyarchy, social proofs (self-selection, rule of 3). Sensing, shaping and seizing.

Arend & Bromiley (2009)

Evaluating dynamic capabilities

TL;DR: four critiques of DC: unclear value-added relative to incumbent concepts; incoherent theory; weak empirical support; unclear practical implications

Girod & Whittington (2017)

Empirical DC

TL;DR: empirical study of large US firms 1985-2004. Pervasive restructuring better than limited reconfiguration generally, but result reversed in dynamic environments

Lieberman & Montgomery (1988)

First-mover advantages

TL;DR: learning-by-doing, patents, switching costs, brand loyalty VS. free-rider, capital inertia, creative destruction

Suarez & Lanzolla (2005)

Half-truth of first mover advantage

TL;DR: short-lived FMA likely in rough waters and when market leads. Long-term FMA likely in calm waters and when market leads. FMA unlikely when technology leads

Shapiro & Varian (1999)
Standards Wars

TL;DR: e.g. Edison vs Westinghouse (tech determined), CBS vs RCA (FCC backs CBS but then tech determines RCA better so FCC switches). Key tactics : pre-emption and expectation management

Markides & Geroski (2004)

Racing to be 2nd

TL;DR: large corporations should focus on consolidating new markets into mass ones, leaving market creation to entrepreneurs. They could consider creating their own small entrepreneurial units

Teece (1986)

Profiting from technological innovation

TL;DR: profits from innovation may → complementary asset owners when imitation is easy; innovators may sometimes need to establish prior asset positions. Market positioning of products is also critical.

Teece (2010)

Business Models, Strategy & Innovation

TL;DR: to profit from innovation, a strong business model (i.e. conceptual framework for how to capture value and generate profit) is required

Buisson & Silberzahn (2010)

Blue Ocean vs Fast Second

TL;DR: neither 'blue ocean' nor 'fast second' guarantees success (== submarket domination). Four-breakthrough framework: tech, business model, design, process

DiMaggio & Powell (1983)

Institutional isomorphism

TL;DR: three isomorphic processes: coercive, mimetic, normative (== external pressure, uncertainty, legitimacy). Dependent on: dependency on resources/other organisations, uncertainty, unions and academics

Oliver (1991)

Strategy and institutions

TL;DR: strategic responses to institutional processes

Zhao, et al. (2017)

Optimal distinctiveness

TL;DR: current approaches to optimal distinctiveness regard differentiation and conformity (e.g. geography, institutions, products) as mutually exclusive, research should identify the process as more complex and fragmented

Deephouse (1999)

Strategic balance

TL;DR: empirical support for strategic balance (between differentiation and conformity) is found in study of commercial banks: curvilinear ROA from strategic deviation

Palich, et al. (2000)

Curvilinearity in diversification

TL;DR: three decades of research (= 82 studies) support a curvilinear model of diversification performance, usually through accounting measures but some limited market measures also

Nippa, et al. (2011)

Corporate portfolio management

TL;DR: CPM has fallen out of fashion since the 80s, due to misapplication (assuming prescriptive power). Critiques include oversimplification, definitional ambiguity, over-focus on industry. Some academics argue diversification destroys value at all levels

Purkayastha, et al. (2012)

Emerging Market Diversification

TL;DR: related diversification in developed economies (specific resources), but unrelated diversification in emerging economies (generic resources) as can develop internal institutions and spread fixed costs of diversification

Bettis & Prahalad (1995)
The dominant logic

TL;DR: the dominant logic describes how firms process information inputs, and is the 'genetic' context by which it undertakes all its operations, one level up from organisational learning (feedback loop)

Khanna & Palepu (2008)

Indian Diversification

TL;DR: in Indian business groups (1300 firms, public data), performance (accounting + stock market) declines initially but most diversified groups outperform all other categories, due to access to foreign capital, concentrated owner monitoring, internal markets

Anand, et al. (2009)

Multimarket contact

TL;DR: differentiate between multimarket exploitation (= value through mutual forbearance) vs exploration (= reduce risk through mimetic entry and exit). Supported by 19 biomed firms 1988-1998

Porter (1987)

Corporate strategy

TL;DR: in top 33 US companies (1950-1986), more than 60% of acquisitions divested (74% for unrelated industries). 3 tests: industry attractiveness, better-off, cost-of-entry

Moeller, et al. (2005)

Acquisition returns

TL;DR: acquirer shareholders lose large amounts on announcement, esp. in 1998-2001 where 12% was lost immediately. This is usually offset by gains from the acquiree, but not in those 3 years due to extremely high valuations. Large losses correlate with poor subsequent performance

Hayward & Hambrick (1997)

CEO Hubris

TL;DR: in 106 large acquisitions, correlation between premium paid and: recent acquirer performance, recent media praise for CO, measure of CEO self-importance. Esp. true when board is mostly inside directors / CEO is chair. ↑ Hubris → ↑ shareholder losses

Barney (1988)

M&A & RBV

TL;DR: M&A are only successful when acquirers can capture either: private, inimitable or unexpected synergistic cashflows

Capron & Pistre (2002)

Abnormal returns from M&A

TL;DR: in 101 horizontal acquisitions (range of industries & countries), abnormal returns only generated when acquirer transfers resources to acquiree - vice versa implies competitors could have done the same and thus profits are competed away. Value creation != value capture for acquirer

Puranam & Chaudhuri (2009)

Integrating Acquisitions

TL;DR: structural integration can be used to achieve coordination post-M&A. This can be costly, so preexisting common ground is an alternate route to achieve the same goal. Empirical support from 217 acquisitions by 49 firms in US high-tech

Trichterborn, et al. (2016)

M&A learning

TL;DR: dedicated M&A functions with explicit learning processes (articulation, codification, sharing, internalisation) can improve M&A performance. Empirical support from 751 German firms via survey

Fridolfsson & Stennek (2017)

Preemptive Mergers

TL;DR: mergers might reduce profits but raise share prices due to preemptive merging to exclude rivals from potential benefits. Economic model derives result, showing in equilibrium total share value can increase when profits fall

Dyer, et al. (2004)

Ally or Acquire

TL;DR: acquire when reciprocal / hard-resource / rationalisation synergies or rivals, ally when modular / sequential / soft-resource (e.g. labour) synergies or high uncertainty

Yin & Shanley (2008)

Merger vs Alliance

TL;DR: M&A vs alliance depend on: industry demands for commitment, environmental pressures for flexibility, choice availability from concentration and institutions

Capron & Mitchell (2010)

Finding the right path

TL;DR: answer tree - relevant resources → internal development; elif agreement of resource value → purchase contract; elif closeness undesired → alliance; else acquire.

Christoffersen (2013)

Synthesis of Intl. Alliances

TL;DR: antecedents of performance in international alliances - commitment, trust, co-operation, conflict, size differences, culture clash, relatedness, experience, dominant control. Many different metrics used but little note of their significance in studies.

Van Alstyne, et al. (2016)

Pipelines & Platforms

TL;DR: IT advances have made platforms the dominant model, harnessing network effects to change strategy - resource orchestration, external interaction, ecosystem value.

McIntyre & Srinivasan (2016)

Networks & Platforms

TL;DR: platforms can be viewed as value generators of network effects. Contributions from IO (direct vs indirect NE), strategy (e.g. FMA vs dominant design entry), tech management

Gnyawali & Park (2011)

Co-opetition between giants

TL;DR: co-opetition is difficult but very beneficial for tackling technological challenges (case study: Samsung & Sony 2003-09 S-LCD, $3bn each, concerns of growing TV competition). Co-opetition between giants cause subsequent rival co-opetition (LG-Toshiba, Hitachi-Panasonic, etc.).

Powell (1990)

Neither market nor hierarchy

TL;DR: networks are fundamentally different to markets & hierarchies: relational communication, reciprocal conflict resolution, reputation, open-ended tone

Oxley (1997)

Alliances & Transaction Costs

TL;DR: alliances can be incorporated into the market-hierarchy continuum using TCE. Factors: design vs production, product range, geographical reach, no. firms, pre-existing alliances. Empirical evidence from 165 public US manufacturing alliances 1980-1989

Adler (2001)

Market, Hierarchy and Trust

TL;DR: price and authority omit trust-driven community, essential as knowledge economy expands

         Knowledge expands with use → increasing importance (education, scientific frontiers)

o    Knowledge and hierarchy: bad at innovation as authority mode → routinisation

o    Knowledge and markets: public good so underprovided (should be free)

         Trust → community = informal relationships (as opposed to faceless spot market transactions)

         Role in post-capitalist society? Requires far greater levels of trust, esp. in government

Ghoshal & Moran (1996)
Bad for practice: A critique of the transaction cost theory

TL;DR: TC economics focuses too much on market failure and opportunism, which fundamentally undermines its utility in practice.

         The tiger story: selfish, single-goal driven. Not reflective?

         Williamson's model is based around firms existing due to market failures - especially opportunism

o    In reality, there are many efficient and productive firms - our economy may be comprised of organisations over markets (Simon's organizational economy). Opportunism is not rampant, due to long-term nature and trust - link to Adler or Coase?

         Rumelt: firms replace markets when nonmarket means of coordination and commitment are superior

o    Markets possess autonomous adaptation vs organisations creating purposive adaptation

         Williamson ignores the dynamic nature of efficiency (think Schumpeter and innovation)

o    In general, TC theory leaves little room for adaptation or incorporation of more nuanced factors

Ritala, et al. (2014)

Amazon Coopetition

TL;DR: case study of Amazon coopetition through i) Marketplace, ii) AWS (incl. Netflix), iii) Apple-Amazon digital texts, despite initial concerns from incumbent stakeholders.

Pascale (1996)

The Honda Effect

TL;DR: the classic Honda story. Miscalculation, serendipity and organizational learning. Coming this fall, only on NBC.

Hondas highly successful entry into the US motorcycle market was based on miscalculation, serendipity, and organizational learning. Their eventual breakthrough into the previously untapped general-public market with their small 50cc bikes came about not through deliberate plan. Instead, it was largely in reaction to unexpected interests in unconventional distribution channels and to problems with their larger, macho-orientated bikes which is what they had initially intended to sell. Thus the supposed Honda Effect is one of adaptability to new opportunities, of experimentation and learning.

Mintzberg (1996)

Learning 1, Planning 0

TL;DR: The Honda story is a champion of incrementalism over rationality, the "one true data point" available in the debate

Goold (1996)

Design, Learning and Planning: A Further Observation on the Design School Debate

TL;DR: incrementalists focus too much on Honda's experience rather than what can be learned, which is where formal planning is useful. Strategic planning identifies areas of interest for managerial experimentation

Mintzberg (1994)

The Fall and Rise of Strategic Planning

TL;DR: Mintzberg fucking hates formal planning.

         Formal planning is calculating (fixed destination, workers commanded towards goal), not committing (workers shape journey)

         Strategic planning stifles strategic thinking by encouraging automation and routinisation over creativity and adaptability

         Fallacies of strategic planning

o    Prediction: planning requires impossible knowledge of the future to structure itself

o    Detachment: planning encourages separation of thinker and doer, meaning managers never understand the processes from the bottom-up (contrary to Taylor's original statements). An obsession with data over experience is dangerous

o    Formalization: rational sequences cannot internalize, comprehend and synthesise information

         Uses of planning

o    Strategic programming (deployment)

o    Communication and control tools

o    Strategy finders (discovering unobserved markets, new processes)

o    Catalysts

Gavetti & Rivkin (2007)

Origin of Strategy

TL;DR: strategy can be modelled over time - cognitive + physical elements become ↓ plastic while rationality ↑, creating tension between understanding and exploiting surrounding environment

Bromiley & Papenhausen (2003)

Rationality & Equilibrium in Strategy

TL;DR: use of rational behaviour and equilibrium market assumptions do not work well with strategic management. Behavioural economic models are ineffective, should move towards Carnegie model. Example from RBV rationalism (assume resource boundedly optimised), should unpair resource from performance

Kahneman & Lovallo (1993)

Choices & Forecasts

TL;DR: isolating problems as unique neglect historical statistics and future opportunities. Risk-aversion ignores effects of statistical aggregation. Inside views and anchoring on plans → over-optimism.

Mintzberg & Waters (1994)

Of strategies, deliberate and emergent

TL;DR: planned, entrepreneurial, ideological, umbrella, process, unconnected, consensus, imposed. PEIUPUCI.

Sibony, et al. (2017)

Behavioural Strategy

TL;DR: reductionist (beh. econ., individual), pluralist (statistical, groups), contextualist (qualitative, worldviews), problems with prevalent reductionist (more than sum of parts). Investment (risk), resource allocation (agility), blue sky (innovation). Formality, layering, info., participation, incentives, debate, closure (FLIPDIC)

Langley (1988)

Roles of Formal Strategy

TL;DR: formal planning cannot provide vision nor direction, but can play peripheral roles: public relations (impress outsiders), information (provide input), group therapy (dissemination, consensus - impt.!), direction and control (implementation).

Grant (2003)

Strategic Planning in a Turbulent Environment

TL;DR: 12 oil firms in turbulent 70s - all used strategic planning, but with corporate only providing direction, inputs came from divisions and units

Burgelman (1991)

Ecology of Strategy Making

TL;DR: Intel case study, where individual units are encouraged to innovate autonomously, management implements successful strategies in wider context

Mirabeau & Maguire (2013)

From autonomous strategic behavior to emergent strategy

TL;DR: independent units develop new procedures, which are mobilised, legitimised and embedded to become emergent strategy

Brews & Hunt (1999)

Learning to plan and planning to learn: Resolving the debate

TL;DR: 656 firms - heavy synthesis of planned-emergent strategy, even in "Very Unstable environment[s]"

Bach & Allen (2010)

Nonmarket Strategy Briefing

TL;DR: nonmarket strategy is very important. Consider information, coalitions, consistency, uncertainty, values, esp. with globalisation. (IA)3: issue, actor, interests, arena, information, assets

Dorobantu, et al. (2017)

Institutional Nonmarket Strategy

TL;DR: firms can either adapt to, augment or transform weak institutions using: internalisation, partnership, proactive, collective, influence, or coalition strategies (2 for each, independent vs collaborative). Choice depends on whether institutions are incomplete or captured

Faccio (2006)

Politically Connected Firms

TL;DR: in global inspection of 20,202 firms in 47 countries, political connections more likely in nations that: are corrupt, impose restrictions on foreign investments, are more transparent (may be more info available)

Holburn & Vanden Bergh (2014

Political campaigns & integrated strategies

TL;DR: for US electric firms 1998-2006, campaign contributions increase the year before a merger, more so in states with greater political party competition

Baron (1995)

Integrated strategy: market and non-market components

TL;DR: incorporate non-market strategies e.g. overturn import duties (Cemex 58% → 31%), protection against dumping, overturn Large-Scale Retail Store Laws for foreign firms (Toys 'R Us Japan)

         Non-market = social, political and legal arrangements

         Consider 5-forces framework, both within and as a 6th force

Doh et al. (2012)

Advancing nonmarket strategy research: Institutional perspectives in a changing world

TL;DR: institutional perspectives will become increasingly important for NMS research, which is currently disparate and non-unified

Kurshitashvili (2008)

US vs EU Lobbying

TL;DR: EU lobbying system is underdeveloped vs US, since it follows non-compulsory Code of Conduct and lacks transparency in many regions
