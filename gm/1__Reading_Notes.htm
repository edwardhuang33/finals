Edward Huang

 

Chandler (1977)

The Visible Hand

TL;DR: technology-based managerial revolution

         Survey of every firm with >$20 million assets

         Firms accumulated in industries where management replaced market in coordination (not generation)

         Causes: technological (trains, telegraph, coal), creation of managerial hierarchy

         Three stages: market (1790-1840); shift to manager through tech (1840-1880); acceleration through train and telegraph (1880-1920)

         Rise of M-form (1920s) briefly considered, but more Strategy and Scope (1962)

         Key investments in production, distribution and managers

Chandler (1990)

The Enduring Logic of Industrial Success

TL;DR: invest in production, management, marketing and distribution in related industries

         Managerial enterprise = "large industrial concerns in which operating and investment decisions are made by a hierarchy of salaried managers governed by a board of directors"

         Adv. of size: econ. of scale + scope, marketing and distribution, formation of managerial hierarchy

         Historically large first mover advantage

         Examples of harnessing scale

         Rockefeller: 40 firms → Standard Oil trust

o    Cost dropped >80% in 6 years

         Bayer, BASE and Hoechst: dyes and pharmaceuticals (econ. of scope)

o    Cost dropped >95%

o    Overtook Britain who failed to made "the essential investments in production, distribution, and management"

o    Steady flow, marketing, distribution, management

         IBM: investment in R&D, production, marketing, management

o    Outdated? "Major players in the industry have changed little in the past decade" may no longer be true

         When large is not logical:

         Stagnating management (Ford firing his best executives as GM & Chrysler evolved)

         1960s diversification: lacked knowledge, reliance on financial data

o    1973-1977, 1/2 of all M&A assets were acquired in unrelated industries

John (1997)

Elaborations, revisions, dissents: Alfred D. Chandler, Jr.'s, The Visible Hand after twenty years

TL;DR: VH is influential and acclaimed, but is nevertheless criticised and at times dismissed

         Historical context of VH

         Personal background

o    Patrician upbringing, exposure to business leaders, editor of wartime Eisenhower papers

o    WWII navy experience: organization, respect for US big business

o    Harvard Business School 70s-80s

         Prior works

o    Timelines: origin of big business → railroad, origin of industrial revolution → coal, both decades later than previous theses

o    Subversion of British role: US managerial role given more import

         Effects of VH

o    Widespread influence: "Virtually every work now written on the history of modern, large-scale enterprise must begin by placing itself within the Chandlerian analytical framework" (Porter, 1992)

o    Use of "Chandlerian"

o    Focus on big business, marginalised analysis of state / SME

         Chandler kept narrow focus on internal business dynamics, minimally on cultural/social factors

 

         Champions

         Colleagues and students at HBS

o    Extension in business role in advancing technology (Carlson, 1991): GE

o    Extension into history of mass marketing (Tedlow, 1990): origins of mass marketing in transport and communication revolutions

         Critics

         Economic vitality of early republic: "entrepreneurial attitudes and strategies of upward economic mobility" (Perkins, 1989)

         Neglect of canal, postal and political systems (Cochran, 1981)

         Role of state: military (influence on telegraph system), federal banks, post (information market)

         Importance of market control instead of efficiency gains

         Social perspective

o    Strategy by corporate liberal to maintain power (Sklar, 1988)

o    Rise of middle management as portion of workforce (Zunz)

o    Worker control (obstructing restructuring, establishment of job ladders, creation of welfare programs i.e. corporate paternalism)

         Skeptics

         Omission of non-capital-intensive industries and small business (Blackford, 1991)

Hamilton & Biggart (1988)

Market, culture, and authority: A comparative analysis of management and organization in the Far East

TL;DR: Big Japan, state Korea, small Taiwan. Economic + cultural explanations explain growth well, but authority explanation is best for understanding market structure.

         Countries

         Japan

o    2nd largest GNP in world 1984

o    Two types of interfirm networks

                                 i.        Loose linkages amongst large firms: horizontal, equal treatment, intermarket

  Kigyo shudan: e.g. Mitsubishi, groups of firms in unrelated industries joined by central banks / trading comapnies

                               ii.         SME-to-large firm links: vertical, unequal

  Typically, large firms handle raw materials, SMEs execute processing (not cars, though)

         Domination by central, large firms

         South Korea

o    >50% growth p/a exports 1963-1972

o    Large, hierarchical networks (chaebol)

         Top 5 controlled 8.8% of GNP (1973)

o    Within network, each firm is controlled by individual/family, but has less control vs Japan as they are directly managed by SK state

         Taiwan

o    >10% growth p/a GNP 1963-1972

o    Family firm and business group dominant, low levels of integration and oligarchy, some smaller family-owned business networks

         60% workforce employed in SMEs in 1976 (Zhao, 1982)

         Frameworks explaining organizational patterns

         Economic

o    Chandlerian efficiency, Williamsonian transaction-cost

         All three have well-developed transport and communication systems, access to advanced tech

o    Fits moderately well with Japanese large multi-unit firms (but not causally as predates tech), somewhat with SK vertically integrated but state controlled chaebol (ditto), and not at all with SME Taiwan (despite high uncertainty and acquisitions in unrelated expanding markets)

         Cultural

o    "socially learned way of life of a people and the means by which orderliness and patterned relations are maintained in a society"

o    Japanese wa (harmony) → movement amongst divisions for ↑ understanding, consultation with colleagues for contract negotiations, seniority systems (but high turnover rates 20s-30s, so perhaps economic explanations better here)

o    Chinese Confucianism: self-control, obedience to superiors esp. family. Possible → why Chinese conduct business in familial network, but too broad and underlying (Taiwan vs. Japan, both Confucian)

         → Chinese "nesting box": core inheriting family; relatives; professionals; unskilled

o    Explains commonalities but not differences, too broad for specific historical understanding

         Authority

o    All firms have a pattern of command and compliance

o    State power on firms

         SK: strong state influence in economy (e.g. credit rationing) esp L/M sizes, which are preferred. ← Korean war led to need for strong Confucian state

         Japan: creator of autonomous intermarket groups, acts only as mediator and coordinator ← weak power of emperor to devolved state power analogue

         Taiwan: minimal, "virtually free trade conditions" ← CKS updated Confucianism, benevolent rule, "leaves the people at rest"

         Economic & cultural best for understanding growth, but authority best for structure as synthesis of superficial economic and broad cultural factors that is situationally adaptable

TL;DR: large firms exist to gain market control more than for efficiency gains

         In reality, efficiency gains may have been sub-strategies, part of a market takeover / monopolisation manoeuvre

Fligstein (2008)

Chandler and the Sociology of Organizations

TL;DR: Chandler overemphasises managers; the state had a large role to play in causing vertical integration due to illegalisation of cartels

         Chandler is fundamentally Weberian

o    Managers have specific strategies (goals) that define structure (bureaucracy)

         Population ecology: Chandler only examines the large survivors, so overemphasises the role of managers (discounting luck and environmental factors)

         The Sherman Act illegalised cartels in the US in 1980 → firms forced to reduce competition through vertical integration rather than cartels forming (as they did in Germany, France, Britain)

o    In the decade from 1895 (the merger movement), 75% of M&A were horizontal

o    Best predictor of merger was whether there had been a cartel attempt 20 years earlier

Weber (1922)

Economy and Society

TL;DR: Rational bureaucracy is inevitable but nevertheless most superior structure - although beware of depersonalisation, inflexibility and power abusers

         Bureaucracy is a form of specialized administration that has risen out of rational organisation (n.b. this is a Weberian ideal type - constructed as the archetype of observations)

         Activities are distributed

         Power is hierarchical (but right of appeal)

         Roles and decisions are governed by abstract, stable, complete and permanently-recorded rules

         Officials are selected on qualification, appointed not elected, and compensated by salary

         Employment is a life-time career

         Personal property is separate from office property (cf Marx), but Weber sees this as not as a result of capitalism, but generally an inevitable phenomenon in all circles and in all political systems (the "Iron Cage")

         The rational-legal form is the most stable and superior version: reliable, subordinate autonomy, rules-based challenges → more complex capabilities

         Problems

         Depersonalisation of worker → part of larger machine

         Inflexible in the specific case: does away with irrational considerations (e.g. judge)

         Problems of power esp. in state roles

Williamson (1981)

The economics of organization: The transaction cost approach

TL;DR: asset specificity (locational, physical, human), frequency, uncertainty (volatility, measurement of human productivity) determine boundaries of firm

         "A transaction occurs when a good or service is transferred across a technologically separable interface"

         Critical dimensions: uncertainty, frequency, asset specificity (most important)

         Asset specificity types: locational, physical and human. Locks in bilateral symmetrical agreement

         ↓ asset specificity → production scale economies: EoS, aggregation ↓ risk, ↓ DoS

         ↑ asset specificity → governance cost economies: ↓ opportunism, ↓ contracting costs, ↓ cost to resolve conflict, ↑ access to relevant information

         Human asset specificity

         Human asset specificity: transferability of skill if employment relation is severed

         Uncertainty → extent to which productivity can be measured / assessed

o    e.g. two men lifting cargo into truck, MP cannot be measured only through output data (Alchian & Demsetz, 1972)

 

 

Human asset specific

Not specific

Easy to measure

Internal spot market. Relation ends when either party is dissatisfied.

Obligational market. Idiosyncratic tech / firm-specific knowledge (accounting, specialised machinery) → safeguards against dismissal, seniority benefits

Hard to measure

Primitive team. e.g. cargo team. Teams can change but individuals cannot be justifiably compensated

Relational team. Schemes to deepen understanding of firm, ↑ job security. Rare, possibly in Japan

 

         Unions: under efficiency (not power) considerations, industries with high human transaction costs → early development of unions (e.g. railroad). Employers will be more accepting of unions in these conditions vs. those where power is sought

         Links

         Hannan & Freeman's (1977) question "Why are there so many kinds of organizations?" "little attention is paid to issues concerning the proper units of analysis"

         Simon: bounded rationality, only reason for inefficient contracting and hence need to decide between market and internal governance. Creation of divisional form to harness boundaries of rationality and create order

TL;DR: technological progress → ↓ urgency of buffering → ↓ need for Chandlerian firm

         The managerial revolution was a impermanent solution to an imbalance in factors contributing to Smith's forces of specialisation (tech vs organisation vs institution)

o    As markets change and institutions evolve, the need for such large firms lessens

         e.g. dynamic transaction costs: evolving markets are better able to support new technological processes

         Internet? May → disaggregation, but depends if it lowers external TC more than internal

Ghoshal & Moran (1996)
Bad for practice: A critique of the transaction cost theory

TL;DR: TC economics focuses too much on market failure and opportunism, which fundamentally undermines its utility in practice.

         The tiger story: selfish, single-goal driven. Not reflective?

         Williamson's model is based around firms existing due to market failures - especially opportunism

o    In reality, there are many efficient and productive firms - our economy may be comprised of organisations over markets (Simon's organizational economy). Opportunism is not rampant, due to long-term nature and trust - link to Adler or Coase?

         Rumelt: firms replace markets when nonmarket means of coordination and commitment are superior

o    Markets possess autonomous adaptation vs organisations creating purposive adaptation

         Williamson ignores the dynamic nature of efficiency (think Schumpeter and innovation)

o    In general, TC theory leaves little room for adaptation or incorporation of more nuanced factors

Pascale (1996)

The Honda Effect

TL;DR: the classic Honda story. Miscalculation, serendipity and organizational learning. Coming this fall, only on NBC.

Hondas highly successful entry into the US motorcycle market was based on miscalculation, serendipity, and organizational learning. Their eventual breakthrough into the previously untapped general-public market with their small 50cc bikes came about not through deliberate plan. Instead, it was largely in reaction to unexpected interests in unconventional distribution channels and to problems with their larger, macho-orientated bikes which is what they had initially intended to sell. Thus the supposed Honda Effect is one of adaptability to new opportunities, of experimentation and learning.

The Fall and Rise of Strategic Planning

TL;DR: Mintzberg fucking hates formal planning.

         Formal planning is calculating (fixed destination, workers commanded towards goal), not committing (workers shape journey)

         Strategic planning stifles strategic thinking by encouraging automation and routinisation over creativity and adaptability

         Fallacies of strategic planning

o    Prediction: planning requires impossible knowledge of the future to structure itself

o    Detachment: planning encourages separation of thinker and doer, meaning managers never understand the processes from the bottom-up (contrary to Taylor's original statements). An obsession with data over experience is dangerous

o    Formalization: rational sequences cannot internalize, comprehend and synthesise information

         Uses of planning

o    Strategic programming (deployment)

o    Communication and control tools

o    Strategy finders (discovering unobserved markets, new processes)

o    Catalysts

Simon (1991)

Organizations and Markets

TL;DR: bounded rationality, organisation economy, workers for the firm, coordination for standardisation.

         Neoclassical economics fails to answer why employees can be induced to work more than minimally, and work to increase the profits of the firm (not necessarily to their gain)

         Principal-agent: it is unlikely that top managers have a utility function dependent on profits rather than bonuses, quiet life, etc.

         New institutional economics draws little on actual empirical work to support their increased number of assumptions

         In reality, the economy is comprised primarily of organisations with the market linking between then rather than vice versa → organisation economy

         How to motivate workers to perform in the interests of the firm?

         Incomplete contracts: specify agreement, but not manner in which to act to reach end goals

o    May be insufficiently defined e.g. "Always act in so as to maximise the profit of the firm"

         Performance measures and rewards

o    Can be difficult to identify individual performance

         One solution is divisionalisation

         Most importantly, loyalty and identification with the firm

o    Evolutionary theory wards off altruism, but humans are not entirely windowless and form part of a society, thus the best-fit are those that can work within a group

         Best performing firms have employees most willing to assume responsibility for producing results, not just "following the rules"

         Coordination's primary activity within a firm is to provide standardised expectations and norms - for procedures, rules and roles

         While inter-firm coordination is entirely economic, intra-firm coordination can draw on loyalty → employees may work beyond self-interested economic boundaries

Cyert & March (1963)

A Behavioural Theory of the Firm

TL;DR: Firms are groups with goals, they negotiate and satisfice

         Most (large) firms are comprised of several groups: managers, shareholders, workers, suppliers, etc.

         To keep various groups satisfied, costs are often above the efficient minimum. The excess = organisational slack (plays stabilising, adaptive role)

o    e.g. high dividends, excessive salaries, prices below profit maximising

         Firms generally possess five goals: production, inventory, market share, sales and profit.

         They often exhibit satisficing behaviour rather than maximisation

         The balance between the goals is negotiated between the groups

         Implementation of goals occurs at two levels: upper and lower management

         Assessment of goals involves a) a financial measure, and b) an improvement measure - the goal must improve the health of the firm

o    Information-gathering for this can be costly

Whittington (2001)

TL;DR: above.

1.     Classical = rational, profit-maximising, deliberate. Assumes predictable environment. Long-term. Morgan's machine metaphor. e.g. Taylorism

2.     Systematic = deliberate, multi-goal orientation. Rooted in local socio-economic environment, plays the rules. Morgan's culture metaphor. e.g. Korean growth and market share strategy

3.     Evolutionary = market determines best strategy, largely irrespective of internal structure and resources. Morgan's organism metaphor. e.g. contingency theory

4.     Processual = bottom-up strategy formation. Learning school, high uncertainty and politicking. Morgan's psychic and political metaphors. e.g. conflict strategy process
